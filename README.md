# Lumen Clean Co.

## Competitive Research

- **Handy:** White/light neutral palette with no emotional color commitment — clean but sterile. Hero uses task-oriented service images (rooms being cleaned, furniture being assembled) rather than lifestyle or aspiration. Social proof is below the fold, buried under feature copy. Does well: categorical service navigation is frictionless and logically grouped. Reads template-y: the "Our Partners" section is visually underdeveloped — placeholder GIFs standing in for real brand logos, giving the page an unfinished skeleton feel.

- **Maid Sailors:** Navy blue, white, and gold — a traditional corporate-trust palette that signals reliability but zero premium differentiation. Hero uses real lifestyle photography of cleaners in uniform in lived-in spaces (kitchens, balconies), which humanizes the service. Social proof is above the fold — "700+ 5-Star Google Reviews" appears in the hero section, front-loaded for credibility. Does well: the three-step booking explainer ("Select → Pick a Date → Enjoy") reduces friction and reinforces the "60-second booking" claim. Reads template-y: the FAQ section follows a verbatim service-industry formula — "Do you bring your own supplies?" and similar questions with no brand voice or personality distinguishing them from any other cleaning site.

- **The Cleaning Authority:** Dark/light contrast neutral palette (black logo, white backgrounds, gray secondaries) — functional but anonymous. Hero leans on category icons (Bathrooms, Kitchens, Living Areas) with no actual photography above the fold; placeholder transparent GIFs stand in for lifestyle imagery. Social proof is below the fold — testimonials live on a separate `/reviews/` page with no pull-quote or star rating surfaced on the homepage. Does well: the sticky "Call for a Free Estimate" CTA with phone number repeated across header, hero, and footer is a high-conversion pattern that removes all friction for phone-preferring customers. Reads template-y: the "Part of the Authority Brands Family" scrolling logo strip is boilerplate corporate filler — it dilutes the brand identity rather than reinforcing the customer value proposition.

- **GetMaid:** Site was unreachable at time of research (connection refused, April 2026). Based on web archive and industry review sources, GetMaid used a light blue/white palette with orange CTA buttons — the default "trustworthy service" color formula ubiquitous across the cleaning industry. Social proof placement was below the fold. Does well: straightforward instant-quote flow. Reads template-y: heavy reliance on stock photography of smiling cleaners in identical poses, indistinguishable from dozens of competing sites.

- **Anti-pattern #1:** Every competitor defaults to blue, white, or navy as their primary palette — the generic "trust and cleanliness" shorthand. Lumen subverts this entirely: cream base, sage green accent, terracotta CTAs. Warmth and luxury over clinical sterility.

- **Anti-pattern #2:** Social proof universally lives below the fold or on separate review pages, treated as an afterthought. Lumen surfaces a single, specific, named testimonial above the fold — credibility as a design element, not a checkbox.

- **Anti-pattern #3:** Hero imagery across all four sites is either task-focused (the work being done) or stock-photo generic (smiling cleaners in uniform). Lumen's hero centers the customer's transformed environment — warm, editorial, Kinfolk-magazine-style photography of the space after, not the labor during.

- **Anti-pattern #4:** Booking flows and CTAs universally use transactional language: "Get a Quote," "Book Now," "Call for a Free Estimate." Lumen uses intentional, unhurried language aligned with its premium positioning — language that implies the customer is choosing an experience, not purchasing a commodity.
